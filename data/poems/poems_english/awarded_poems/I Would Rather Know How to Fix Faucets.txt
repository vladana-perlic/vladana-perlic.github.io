I Would Rather Know How to Fix Faucets

it is natural to envy now and then, a little.
one may envy, for example, a sleeping cat,
because it does not have to get up early
or plumbers, who, if the porn industry
is to be believed, have the most exciting
sex lives.
one envies swallows, because they are the smile
that escaped some old love never recovered from
and became a bird.
one envies, for example,
butterflies because they will never have to
remember youth
or spend eternity as old men in heaven.
one envies poems because only the girls who hum them
are more beautiful than they are,
bees because they die the instant
they wound a man,
goldfish because they do not keep grudges,
trees because they remember everything,
and still stand firm over our heads.
one envies everyone who loves on bridges,
for whom being soaked to the skin is nothing,
a new baptism of their love,
for whom snow is one more blanket
to wrap their trembling bodies in.
one envies in haste and carelessly,
one envies standing up, on the move,
in fast-food restaurant parking lots,
the thousands and thousands of people who
parade through our lives and streets,
a student's cigarette burning down on
lips that are not merely lips.
one envies everything that can leap over
my watch and come into your mind.
only poets,
however beautiful their work,
are never envied.
for poets one can only
weep and wish with all one's strength
that they had never had to write
a single letter.
